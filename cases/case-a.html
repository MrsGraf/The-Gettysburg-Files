STILL AVAILABLE BACKGROUND

The United States had been founded on July 4, 1776, with liberty and equality among its central ideals. Yet slavery remained legal and deeply embedded in parts of the country. By the 1850s, conflict over slavery — especially its expansion into new territories — was intensifying.

HISTORICAL CHAIN RECOVERED
1860 Abraham Lincoln is elected president

His presidency would become one of the most consequential in U.S. history, as he led the Union through the Civil War and made the abolition of slavery a central war aim.

December 1860 South Carolina secedes from the Union

It is the first state to leave the still-young Union. Six more states follow before the outbreak of the Civil War; four others join the Confederacy afterwards.

The conflict had become a civil war. For more than two years, Union and Confederate armies fought across the nation. Then, in the summer of 1863, their paths converged near a small town in Pennsylvania: Gettysburg.

RECOVERED FRAGMENT · JANUARY 1863

THE WAR CHANGES

On January 1, 1863, Lincoln's Emancipation Proclamation declared enslaved people in the rebelling states to be free. Preserving the Union remained central, but emancipation was now explicitly part of the Union war effort.

What was the conflict about?

UNION

The United States and the states that remained part of it during the Civil War fought to preserve the Union.

CONFEDERACY

The Southern states that seceded from the United States formed the Confederate States of America.

SLAVERY

Slavery was central to secession and to the conflict over the nation's future.

FOUNDING IDEALS

The language of liberty and equality stood in profound tension with the continued existence of slavery.
